## Safety & Comfort

Tabletop roleplaying often explores intense emotions, conflict, horror, loss, romance, or personal themes. Good safety practices help groups handle those moments collaboratively so everyone at the table can stay engaged without feeling trapped, pressured, or isolated.

Esiana supports safety at two levels:

Platform expectations — shared norms for browsing, joining, and participating in games on Esiana.
Campaign-level guidance — each table’s own boundaries, content warnings, and agreed safety tools, usually listed on the recruitment lobby or in a Safety Guidelines page.

Safety tools are not meant to interrupt storytelling or “police” roleplay. They exist so groups can communicate clearly when something stops being fun, crosses a boundary, or needs adjustment.

## Platform expectations

### When joining games through the directory:

- Read the campaign’s listed themes, content warnings, and safety notes before requesting a seat.
- Respect the boundaries and tone the table has established.
- Do not pressure other players to explain personal discomfort, trauma, or private reasons for using a safety tool.
- Treat pauses, redirects, or check-ins as a normal part of collaborative play—not a disruption or failure.

### You are always allowed to:

- ask questions about themes or boundaries before joining
- step away temporarily
- leave a session early
- decide a table is not a good fit for you

Leaving a table is sometimes the healthiest outcome for everyone involved. Not every campaign will match every player’s comfort level, playstyle, or expectations.

## Respect at the table

Players and DMs are expected to treat one another with respect across recruitment, play, and campaign spaces.

Harassment, discriminatory behavior, deliberate boundary violations, or repeated attempts to make other players uncomfortable are not considered acceptable table conduct.

Disagreements and misunderstandings can happen in collaborative storytelling. The expectation is that groups handle them with communication, empathy, and respect for stated boundaries.

## On each campaign lobby

Recruitment lobbies may include:

- Content warnings — themes, subjects, or tones the DM wants players to know in advance
- Safety tools — the methods the table actively uses during play
- Safety Guidelines — campaign-specific expectations, boundaries, and procedures, often linked under Before you apply

Some tables use formal safety frameworks. Others use lighter conversational approaches. Neither automatically makes a table safer than another—the important thing is that expectations are communicated clearly and respected consistently.

If a lobby does not mention safety practices, it is okay to ask politely how the group handles check-ins, boundaries, or uncomfortable scenes before joining.

## Common safety tools

| Tool | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| **X-Card** | A quick way to pause or redirect uncomfortable content without needing to explain why. Some groups also use related check-in tools such as N cards (when content is approaching a boundary) and O cards (to check whether the group is comfortable continuing). |
| **Lines & Veils** | Lines = never in play; veils = may exist off-screen, not played in detail. |
| **Open Door** | Leave or take a break anytime, no justification required. |
| **Session Zero** | A pre-campaign conversation about tone, expectations, characters, and boundaries. |
| **Consent checklist** | A structured way to discuss themes players are comfortable including in play. |
| **Script Change** | A set of roleplay pacing tools inspired by media controls such as pause, rewind, fast forward, and frame-by-frame. Players can slow down, skip, revisit, or redirect scenes collaboratively when needed. |
| **Luxton Technique** | A safety approach that combines clear content boundaries, check-ins, grounding techniques, and aftercare practices—especially useful for emotionally intense or horror-focused games. |
| **Stars & Wishes** | A reflection tool often used after sessions. Players share “stars” (things they enjoyed) and “wishes” (things they would like to explore, improve, or see more of in future sessions). |

These tools are collaborative aids, not tests or punishments. Different groups use different combinations depending on tone, trust, and experience level.

If something goes wrong

If a scene or interaction becomes uncomfortable:

- use the table’s agreed safety tools
- ask for a pause or redirect
- talk privately with the DM if needed
- step away from the session if necessary

You do not need to remain in a game that consistently ignores boundaries or makes you feel unsafe.

Likewise, misunderstandings can happen even in well-intentioned groups. Most tables benefit from calm communication, clarification, and adjustment rather than immediate escalation.

## Safety and trust

No written guideline can guarantee a perfect table experience. Safety in tabletop play ultimately depends on trust, communication, and mutual respect between real people.

The recruitment directory is meant to help players find tables that communicate their expectations clearly and approach collaborative storytelling in good faith.

For how to browse and join tables, see [Joining Process](/guides/joining-process).

## For DMs and facilitators

Many safety and check-in tools can be adapted for online play using Discord, Foundry, Roll20, Owlbear, and other virtual tabletops.

Future guides will cover practical ways to:
- run Session Zero online
- handle private check-ins
- implement X/N/O cards digitally
- support breaks and Open Door policies
- communicate boundaries during remote play

Safety tools do not need to be complicated to be effective. Clear expectations and consistent communication usually matter more than specific software or frameworks.
